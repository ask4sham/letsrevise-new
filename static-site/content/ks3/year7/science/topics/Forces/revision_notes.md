# Forces - Revision Notes

## Overview
Forces are a fundamental concept in science, describing the push or pull on an object due to interactions with other objects. They can change an object's motion, shape or even its state of rest. Understanding forces is key to grasping many other concepts in physics and the wider world around us.

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this topic, you should be able to:
- Understand and define the different types of forces
- Explain how forces can affect motion
- Calculate resultant forces 
- Understand and explain the effects of friction and gravity
- Apply these concepts to real-world situations

## Key Concepts & Definitions
- **Force**: A push or pull on an object caused by an interaction with another object.
- **Contact Force**: Forces that act at the point of contact between two objects (e.g., friction, air resistance).
- **Non-contact Force**: Forces that act even when the objects are not in contact (e.g., gravity, magnetic force).
- **Resultant Force**: The overall force acting on an object when all individual forces are considered together.
- **Friction**: A force that resists the motion of one surface over another.
- **Gravity**: A force that attracts two objects toward each other.

## Detailed Explanations
1. **Types of Forces**: Forces can be contact (like friction or tension) or non-contact (like gravity or magnetism). The effect of a force depends on its direction and magnitude.
2. **Forces and Motion**: A resultant force acting on an object will cause it to accelerate in the direction of the force. This is described by Newton's First Law of Motion.
3. **Friction and Gravity**: Friction is the force that opposes motion, while gravity is the force that pulls objects towards the centre of the Earth. Both forces can influence an object's motion.

*Example 2:*
Problem: If an object is at rest and the forces acting on it are balanced, what is the resultant force?
Solution: The resultant force is 0. This is because the forces are balanced and there is no net force causing the object to move.

## Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them
- **Mistake**: Confusing the direction of forces. 
  **Solution**: Always clearly define and label the direction of each force.
- **Mistake**: Forgetting that the resultant force on an object at rest or moving at a constant speed is zero.
  **Solution**: Remember Newton's First Law - an object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction, unless acted upon by a resultant force.

## Exam Techniques & Tips
- Use arrows to represent forces in diagrams. The length of the arrow should represent the magnitude of the force.
- Always show your workings in calculations.
- In multiple-choice questions, eliminate clearly wrong answers first to narrow down your options.
- Use the 'F = ma' equation (Force equals mass times acceleration) when the problem involves calculating force, mass, or acceleration.
